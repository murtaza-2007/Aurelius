Named after the Stoic emperor who wrote that every idea connects to the next. Wikipedia is proof of that; millions of articles linked by shared meaning. Aurelius makes those connections visible by finding the path between any two articles, however unrelated they seem.

How it works

Works best on desktop; the live graph and side panel are easiest to read on a larger screen.

Best-First Search
Instead of checking links at random, Aurelius always opens the article that looks closest to the goal next, like always taking the turn that points toward where you're headed. It's the same idea behind A*: chase the most promising lead first rather than exploring everything.
Semantic Embeddings
Each article is turned into a string of numbers that captures its meaning. Related topics get similar numbers, so "Jazz" sits near "Saxophone" but far from "Java". Comparing those numbers is how the search tells whether a link actually gets it closer.
Search From Both Ends
Aurelius also gathers every article that links to your destination ahead of time. When the forward search runs into one of them, the two sides meet in the middle, reaching the answer far quicker than searching from the start alone.
Breaking Out of Dead Ends
If the search keeps circling a cluster of similar-but-wrong pages, it notices it has stopped making progress, casts a wider net, and favors more varied links to escape, and it never invents a link that doesn't truly exist.

Where this is going

Right now Aurelius is a demo; it runs on Wikipedia because it's a huge, familiar graph that's genuinely fun to watch the search crawl through. But the engine underneath isn't tied to Wikipedia: the same idea of finding the meaningful path through a giant network could one day power research-paper discovery, internal knowledge bases, and more. Consider this step one toward a general, deployable tool.
